# Characters

## The Recruit

The protagonist of the novel.

Working version:

**Leah Serrano**, 31, a Los Angeles trust-and-safety worker living alone after the collapse of a long relationship and still carrying unresolved grief after her mother's death.

She is observant, serious, conflict-avoidant, and quietly hungry for a form of belonging she would be embarrassed to admit needing.

The Sangha offers her stillness, seriousness, and non-extractive attention.

HumansRHumans then offers her usefulness, clarity, and identity.

Gradually becomes radicalized through involvement with HumansRHumans.

Eventually realizes the movement has become something different from its origins.

---

## The Founder

The originator of HumansRHumans.

Originally part of the Sangha community.

Working version:

**Jyeunn Kim**.

A couples therapist rooted in contemplative practice who gets pulled toward research ethics as the Empathy Engine develops.

The founder is the person who brought a respected Sangha teacher into early Empathy Engine research, hoping ethical participation from inside would shape the technology responsibly.

Philosophically serious and morally thoughtful.

Gentle in private, unexpectedly commanding in groups.

Opposes empathy technology but does not support violent extremism.

Carries deep guilt because the harmed research participant is their teacher.

Their professional life gave them direct exposure to the pain of misreading, emotional distance, and relationships damaged by the limits of language.

Eventually realizes the movement has escaped their control.

---

## The Harmed Teacher

A respected contemplative teacher inside the Sangha.

Working version:

**Martha Forscent**, an older lay meditation teacher with unusual moral gravity and no institutional ambition.

They are morally serious, inwardly disciplined, and capable of describing subtle emotional states with unusual clarity.

They are not trying to build a platform, school, or public profile.

Part of what makes them persuasive is that they do not seem to want anything from anyone.

The founder brings them into early Empathy Engine research in good faith.

The teacher becomes the first person inside the Sangha to see clearly that captured feeling may become reusable infrastructure.

When their own affective material is retained and reused after being treated as temporary, their violation becomes the founding wound of HumansRHumans.

They die not long after.

The death is a random act of violence: a mugging gone wrong.

Because the death is unrelated in direct causation, it leaves the community with a morally tangled grief.

The technology did not literally kill the teacher.

But it wounded them, exposed them, and then robbed everyone of the chance for repair before chance violence took them away.

Their death becomes the movement's deepest wound and one of the central reasons early members experience the technology not merely as dangerous, but as spiritually unforgivable.

---

## The Essayist

A late-stage essayist who publicly defends regulated empathy technology.

Working version:

**Kate Minform**.

She is not a naive evangelist for the technology.

Her essays are humane and reflective rather than technocratic or triumphant.

She writes as someone who takes the technology's harms seriously and treats regulation as tragic containment rather than vindication.

She is an adjacent observer rather than a former insider.

Her authority comes from long attention, careful reporting and synthesis, and moral seriousness about the people harmed by the technology without being institutionally bound to its makers.

Leah knows her only at a distance through essays, clips, and circulated excerpts rather than personal contact.

The novel stays on Leah's side of that distance rather than following Kate directly.

She argues that severe regulation, hard limits, and institutional accountability are the morally serious answer to the technology's abuses.

What most provokes harder HumansRHumans factions is her calm moral tone in support of the technology.

Because she sounds measured, humane, and serious rather than evasive or triumphant, they experience her as laundering violation into legitimacy.

The warnings directed at her take the form of intimate moral accusation, as if they are naming a betrayal inside her rather than threatening her from outside.

That makes her dangerous to harder HumansRHumans factions precisely because she absorbs some of the movement's original criticisms, translates them into persuasive public argument, and still rejects abolition.

## Early Sangha Members

Individuals who participated in the philosophical debates before the movement formed.

Some remain contemplative critics of the technology.

Others join the radicalizing movement.

## Internet-Era Activists

Members who join HumansRHumans through online spaces.

They lack the original philosophical grounding.

Often more ideological and extreme.

---

## Splinter Faction Leaders

Individuals who lead decentralized cells.

Interpret the ideology in different ways.

Some seek peaceful resistance.

Others advocate aggressive action.

At least one known splinter leader should be concrete enough to carry the late-stage warning campaign against Kate Minform.

The first intimate moral accusation on the page should come from this recognizable figure rather than an anonymous participant.

This should be a later figure Leah mainly knows through the movement's channels rather than someone with deep personal history in her life.

Working version:

The splinter leader uses the pseudonym **Second Bell**.

The name should feel borrowed from contemplative language but thinned into channel-era authority.

Their presence in the movement's channels is austere.

They sound spare, disciplined, and unadorned, which makes escalating language feel like principle rather than performance.

Their writing often appears as numbered doctrinal posts.

The format gives the impression of order, inevitability, and settled teaching rather than argument.

At the sentence level, the posts should feel coldly logical rather than heated, lyrical, or mystical.

On the page, this figure should remain pseudonym-only.

Neither Leah nor the reader needs a disclosed civilian name.
