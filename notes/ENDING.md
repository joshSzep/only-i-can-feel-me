# Ending

Empathy technology becomes regulated.

The original goal of HumansRHumans is achieved.

However the movement does not dissolve.

Splinter factions continue escalating.

The founder attempts to narrow or restrain the movement, but their authority no longer matters in practice.

People still quote the founder.

They no longer obey them.

In the decisive moment, the recruit watches members plan a new campaign of threats framed as warnings against **Kate Minform**, an essayist who publicly defends regulated empathy technology, even after regulation.

The novel remains on Leah's side of that campaign rather than shifting into Kate's direct awareness of it.

The warnings take the form of intimate moral accusation, as if the movement is correcting a betrayal in her rather than issuing threats at all.

They invoke the founder's name and moral language while doing exactly what the founder has told them not to do.

They insist that the founder always cared about interior sovereignty, and that true sovereignty allows no compromise.

The recruit realizes that the movement has evolved beyond its founding purpose.

The founder recognizes the same truth.

The novel ends with the understanding that ideas, once released into the world, cannot easily be contained.
