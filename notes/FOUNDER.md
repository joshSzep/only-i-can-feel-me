# Founder

The founder of HumansRHumans originates from the Sangha community where concerns about empathy technology first emerged.

Working version:

The founder is **Jyeunn Kim**, a couples therapist rooted in contemplative practice who becomes increasingly involved in research ethics as empathy technology develops.

They are close enough to the earliest Empathy Engine development to understand the danger from the inside rather than only as an outside critic.

They are the bridge figure who brought a respected Sangha teacher into the earliest phenomenology and calibration work.

They are contemplative, thoughtful, and philosophically serious.

They are not primarily a technologist.

They come from the world of care.

That matters to the movement's original tone.

## Presence

In private, the founder is gentle, patient, and unusually good at making people feel unhurried.

They listen like a clinician and a contemplative practitioner at once.

In groups, however, they become unexpectedly commanding.

Not theatrical.

Not domineering.

But they can still a room, concentrate attention, and make moral seriousness feel non-optional.

This contrast is one of the reasons people trust them.

Their original belief:

Human consciousness should not be technologically mediated.

They do not initially advocate violence or extremism.

Their goal is to protect the integrity of inner life.

## Background

The founder works as a couples therapist, likely with additional grounding in contemplative counseling or spiritually serious relational care.

They have enough literacy in research and ethics to move between clinic, Sangha, and lab without fully belonging to any one of those worlds.

Their initial reasoning is morally plausible:

if empathy technology is going to exist, then people grounded in suffering, care, and contemplative discipline should help define its ethical limits early.

Their work has made them intimately familiar with one of the strongest arguments in favor of the Empathy Engine:

that relationships often fail not from lack of feeling, but from the inability to transmit feeling accurately enough.

---

## Philosophy

The founder believes:

- empathy should be earned through human relationship
- emotional experience should remain sovereign
- machines should not access human interiority

Their position is grounded in spiritual and philosophical concerns.

It is also shaped by direct exposure to the research culture that helped build the technology.

The founder does not begin as a purist standing outside the system.

They begin as someone who believed ethical involvement from inside might help keep the line from being crossed.

Because they are a couples therapist, they are especially vulnerable to the strongest argument in favor of the technology:

that it might relieve suffering where language, patience, and ordinary care have failed.

It also shapes how they lead.

Their authority does not initially come from ideology or charisma.

It comes from the fact that people have already experienced them as careful, humane, and trustworthy in intimate settings.

---

## Tragedy

Before the first public breakthrough of the Empathy Engine, a Sangha member involved in its early development warns that captured feeling will not remain bounded.

That harmed member is the founder's own teacher.

The teacher is **Martha Forscent**, an older lay meditation teacher with unusual moral gravity and no institutional ambition.

The founder is the person who brought them into the research, believing their teacher's contemplative precision and moral seriousness might keep the work humane.

That warning proves correct when the member's recorded affective material is retained and reused after being treated as temporary.

The founder experiences this not as a technical mistake but as a moral catastrophe.

They also experience it as personal responsibility.

When their teacher is killed not long afterward in a random mugging gone wrong, responsibility hardens into grief and guilt that can no longer be repaired.

The founder knows the technology did not literally cause the death.

What it did cause was the wound that remained unresolved when chance violence took their teacher away.

The founder never fully believes the movement should falsify causality.

But in the earliest public rhetoric, they fail to insist hard enough on the distinction.

They allow the teacher's death to become fused with the movement's indictment of the technology.

That failure becomes one of the movement's first moral compromises.

HumansRHumans begins partly from conviction and partly from guilt.

After the resulting collapse and schism inside the Sangha, the founder helps create HumansRHumans.

The movement begins as a resistance group.

---

## Loss of Control

As HumansRHumans spreads online, the founder gradually loses influence.

New factions emerge.

The ideology becomes more extreme.

The founder eventually realizes the movement has become something they did not intend.

Part of what makes this realization painful is that the movement was born from the founder's attempt to answer a harm they helped make possible.
