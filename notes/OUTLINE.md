# Outline

This is a working structural outline for *Only I Can Feel Me*.

It should remain flexible while characters, the triggering tragedy, and faction specifics are being defined.

The story should stay centered on the recruit's emotional experience of the movement.

The movement must feel like refuge before it feels like danger.

---

## Core Throughline

The recruit enters the story wounded, disoriented, and vulnerable to a community that offers seriousness, belonging, and moral clarity.

The Sangha becomes sanctuary.

The philosophical critique of empathy technology becomes conviction.

The tragic event becomes fracture.

HumansRHumans becomes purpose.

Purpose becomes identity.

Identity becomes compromise.

Compromise becomes disillusionment.

The ending should not suggest that the recruit was foolish to join.

It should show why joining made emotional and moral sense before showing what the movement becomes.

## Chronology Note

The book should begin **before** the opening of *What It Feels Like to Be You*.

Acts I and II unfold during the Empathy Engine's pre-launch development years.

By the time Shelby publicly announces the technology in the first novel, HumansRHumans should already exist in early form.

That announcement accelerates the movement rather than creating it.

---

## Current Working Recruit

**Leah Serrano**, 31.

She lives alone in Los Angeles after the end of a six-year relationship and works in trust and safety for a major platform, where she spends her days reviewing invasive media, private feeling turned into content, and public conflict shaped by emerging empathy technology.

She is observant, serious, conflict-avoidant, and spiritually depleted.

She is grieving her mother, ashamed of how emotionally absent she became before her relationship ended, and increasingly convinced that contemporary life no longer knows how to hold suffering without exploiting it.

The Sangha feels like relief because it offers stillness, non-extractive attention, and a form of seriousness that does not immediately instrumentalize pain.

It is semi-open: accessible enough for Leah to enter through public-facing events, but relationally deep enough that real belonging must be earned slowly.

Her digital fluency later makes her useful to HumansRHumans and helps bind her to the movement more tightly than belief alone would.

More specifically, she becomes useful by moderating online spaces and filtering members.

This gives her both responsibility and complicity.

## Current Working Founder Dynamic

The founder is **Jyeunn Kim**, a couples therapist rooted in contemplative practice who is pulled into research ethics as empathy technology develops.

The founder is the person who brought their own teacher into early Empathy Engine phenomenology and calibration work.

They did so in good faith, believing ethical participation from inside the research was more responsible than refusal from outside.

When the teacher's emotional material is retained and reused after being treated as temporary, the founder experiences the violation as both philosophical catastrophe and personal guilt.

When the teacher is killed not long afterward in a random mugging gone wrong, the founder's guilt becomes unrepairable and helps transform concern into organized resistance.

Because the death is unrelated in direct causation, the movement's founding grief is morally tangled from the beginning.

The first moral compromise emerges here.

Early members know the death was unrelated street violence, but publicly allow it to blur into the movement's indictment of the technology.

This is one of the reasons the founder remains morally serious later.

HumansRHumans begins not from abstraction alone, but from remorse.

The founder's profession matters.

They know how often human beings fail each other inside love, marriage, and long intimacy.

That makes the Empathy Engine look plausible to them before it looks unforgivable.

Their presence matters too.

In private, they feel gentle and trustworthy.

In groups, they become unexpectedly commanding.

That makes them emotionally convincing both as caregiver and as early movement leader.

## Current Working Harmed Teacher

The harmed teacher is **Martha Forscent**, an older lay meditation teacher with unusual moral gravity and no institutional ambition.

They are persuasive not because they seek influence, but because they seem largely indifferent to it.

That makes their warning about captured feeling feel weighty inside the Sangha.

It also makes their violation and death easier for the movement to mythologize.

## Current Working Essayist

**Kate Minform** becomes the late-stage essayist who publicly defends regulated empathy technology.

She is not a simple technophile or restorationist.

Her essays are humane and reflective, which makes her harder to dismiss as a propagandist for the technology.

She is an adjacent observer rather than a former insider, which lets harder factions read her as both informed and intolerably legitimate.

Leah knows Kate only at a distance through essays, interviews, and reposted excerpts, which makes it easier for the movement to turn her into a symbol to be punished.

The story stays on Leah's side of that distance rather than cutting away to Kate's direct experience.

Her public argument is that severe regulation, hard limits, and enforceable accountability are the only morally serious way to contain the technology after its abuses.

What most inflames harder factions is her calm moral tone in support of the technology.

Because she does not sound glib, corporate, or defensive, they read her as morally normalizing what they consider unforgivable.

That makes her an ideal post-regulation target for factions that now treat compromise itself as betrayal.

## Current Working Splinter Leader

**Second Bell** becomes the late-stage splinter leader who gives online extremism its most disciplined voice.

Leah knows this figure mainly through the movement's channels rather than through any deep earlier relationship.

On the page, Second Bell should remain pseudonym-only.

Their presence is austere: severe, disciplined, and stripped of obvious performance.

Their writing usually appears as numbered doctrinal posts.

At the sentence level, those posts should feel coldly logical rather than lyrical, mystical, or slogan-heavy.

That matters because each escalation reads like deduction instead of appetite.

## Drafting Commitments

This file should now function as the primary drafting outline.

Assume close third person through Leah throughout.

Current structural target: 18 chapters split 4 / 4 / 3 / 3 / 4 across the five acts.

Chapter titles are scaffolding, not commitments.

The novel does not leave Leah's side to follow Kate Minform directly.

Second Bell remains pseudonym-only on the page.

## Working Chapter Map

### Act I — Sanctuary

Primary function:
Establish Leah's depletion, the extractive texture of ordinary life, and the Sangha as a credible refuge rather than an obvious recruitment mechanism.

Emotional movement:
Alienation -> refuge

Act priorities:

- make the surrounding world feel administratively corrosive before the novel makes any philosophical argument about empathy technology
- make the Sangha quiet, semi-open, and genuinely relieving rather than mystified or glamorous
- establish Jyeunn as both gentle in private and unexpectedly authoritative in groups
- establish Martha as moral gravity without institutional hunger

1. **Moderation Queue**: Leah moves through trust-and-safety work, grief, and the apartment that still feels recently vacated by her former life. The opening establishes a world where private feeling is already becoming content, product, and problem. Turn: she accepts, or at least does not refuse, the chance to attend a public Sangha gathering.

2. **Open Sit**: Leah enters the Sangha through a public-facing event that feels careful, quiet, and non-extractive. The room itself should feel memorable: silence, ritual, attentiveness, and the relief of not being immediately claimed. Turn: Leah returns, which matters more than any declaration.

3. **Martha Forscent**: Leah begins moving from the public edge toward the slower inner circle. She encounters Jyeunn more closely and meets Martha Forscent, whose lack of ambition gives moral weight to everything she says. Turn: Martha voices the warning that captured feeling will not remain bounded to care.

4. **The Boundary**: The philosophical dispute around empathy technology becomes emotionally real for Leah rather than abstractly interesting. Jyeunn's background as a couples therapist makes the promise of the technology legible even as the danger sharpens. Turn: Leah wants to belong to this community, not merely visit it.

End-of-act turn:
Leah's attachment to the Sangha becomes personal before it becomes ideological.

### Act II — Fracture

Primary function:
Turn contemplative concern into urgent moral crisis and show the movement's first compromise before internet extremism exists.

Emotional movement:
Concern -> conviction

Act priorities:

- deepen Leah's place inside the Sangha so the fracture feels like lived loss
- make the retained-session betrayal specific, not symbolic only
- keep multiple positions sincere inside the community rather than flattening debate
- let grief, guilt, and myth begin mixing before the movement has scaled

5. **Temporary**: Leah becomes more embedded in Sangha rhythms while disagreements sharpen around ethical participation, reform, refusal, and resistance. Martha returns from the research carrying increasingly urgent alarm about retention and reuse. Turn: the possibility emerges that supposedly temporary affective material has already been kept.

6. **Retained**: The retained-session betrayal is confirmed. The chapter should stay with the moral injury of feeling treated as temporary and then converted into reusable material anyway. Turn: Jyeunn's good-faith bridge into the research becomes visible as guilt.

7. **Vigil**: Before the community can metabolize the violation, Martha is killed in a random mugging gone wrong. The vigil or funeral should make grief morally tangled rather than narratively neat. Turn: Leah sees early members allowing the death to blur into the movement's larger indictment of the technology without fully correcting the record.

8. **The First Compromise**: The Sangha can no longer contain its own fracture. In memorial language, private meetings, and early organizing, the first ethical compromise of HumansRHumans takes shape before internet extremism exists. Turn: Leah crosses from witness to participant by choosing the emerging movement.

End-of-act turn:
Leah joins not because she has become simple, but because grief, seriousness, and belonging now point in the same direction.

### Act III — Movement

Primary function:
Show the intoxicating moral energy of early movement life and make Leah's role in it concrete.

Emotional movement:
Conviction -> belonging

Act priorities:

- make early HumansRHumans disciplined and morally serious enough that later drift feels tragic
- define Leah's usefulness through moderation, gatekeeping, and digital judgment
- show online growth as both energizing and simplifying
- let identity harden through labor before overt extremism takes over

9. **Inner Channel**: Leah attends an early HumansRHumans meeting that still feels morally serious rather than fanatic. The founder's original ethics should be clear here: interior sovereignty, restraint, and refusal of violence. Turn: Leah is asked to help with the movement's digital spaces.

10. **Useful**: Leah's moderation and gatekeeping work gives her belonging through labor. She screens newcomers, shapes access, and begins feeling that seriousness can be measured administratively. Turn: usefulness becomes identity.

11. **Admission Criteria**: Online growth outpaces the Sangha's original depth. Slogans begin replacing philosophy, but the movement also becomes more effective, visible, and intoxicating. Turn: Leah notices the first necessity logic entering the channels and does not stop it.

End-of-act turn:
Leah no longer simply belongs to the movement.

She is becoming someone through it.

### Act IV — Escalation

Primary function:
Demonstrate how scale, anonymity, grievance, and purity logic deform the original movement while making Leah's complicity undeniable.

Emotional movement:
Belonging -> compromise

Act priorities:

- distinguish sabotage logic from the founder's earlier ethics
- show channel culture replacing relational trust as the main source of authority
- make Leah's moderating role morally active rather than passive
- reveal that understanding the corruption does not yet free her from it

12. **Pressure**: The sabotage faction argues that empathy technology is an existential threat and therefore damage is justified. Its first major threshold-crossing act is intimidation and targeted harassment of researchers or clinicians, still easy to rationalize as pressure. Turn: Leah moderates the channel where the campaign is coordinated and leaves the campaign itself intact.

13. **Second Bell**: A later, channel-native authority begins to crystallize. Second Bell's numbered doctrinal posts give extremism the cadence of disciplined inference rather than emotional excess, and Leah feels how persuasive cold logic can become when wrapped in moral language. Turn: leadership in practice begins shifting away from relational trust and toward mediated severity.

14. **No Clean Hands**: Jyeunn attempts restraint, but the movement's scale and fragmentation make moral authority increasingly ceremonial. Leah now understands the corruption intellectually, yet leaving feels like self-erasure because the movement supplies identity, intimacy, and structure. Turn: she stays anyway.

End-of-act turn:
Leah understands the corruption clearly enough to name it, but not yet clearly enough to leave.

### Act V — Realization

Primary function:
Deliver the book's central tragic recognition: regulation answered the original political demand, but the movement now persists for reasons deeper and darker than that demand.

Emotional movement:
Compromise -> clarity

Act priorities:

- make regulation feel like apparent victory before revealing it as insufficient ending logic
- keep Kate mediated through essays and channel circulation rather than direct contact
- let Second Bell's coldly logical rhetoric make intimidation sound like moral inference
- make Jyeunn's remaining authority symbolic rather than operative

15. **Regulated**: Empathy technology is finally regulated, briefly appearing to fulfill HumansRHumans' original political aim. Originalists glimpse a possible stopping point, while harder factions immediately recast regulation as weakness. Turn: relief collapses into the clearer fear that success will not end the movement.

16. **Kate Minform**: Leah encounters Kate only through essays, clips, and circulated excerpts. Kate's humane, reflective defense of regulation enrages the harder factions precisely because it sounds morally serious rather than evasive. Turn: Second Bell begins guiding the channel toward threats framed as warnings.

17. **Ceremonial Authority**: The warnings against Kate take the form of intimate moral accusation, delivered in coldly logical numbered posts that read like doctrine rather than menace. Jyeunn attempts to stop the campaign, and Leah finally sees that the founder can still be quoted but no longer obeyed. Turn: the movement's source and its present reality split apart completely.

18. **What Remains**: Leah confronts the fact that HumansRHumans now sustains identity, grievance, and momentum more than principle. The novel does not cut to Kate's direct awareness; it remains with Leah as she understands that the movement meant to defend humanity has learned to preserve its symbols while discarding its restraint. End state: tragic lucidity rather than procedural closure.

End-of-act turn:
Leah sees why the movement began, why it spread, why it corrupted itself, and why it will likely continue.

Most importantly, she sees that the founder's continuing presence is no longer a restraint.

It is now a source of symbolic legitimacy that others can weaponize.

---

## Structural Non-Negotiables

- The Sangha must feel real enough that its loss matters.
- The tragic event must feel specific enough to justify the fracture.
- The founder must remain morally serious, not secretly fraudulent.
- Leah must receive genuine care and meaning from the movement before its corruption becomes undeniable.
- Online growth must visibly strip context from the founding philosophy.
- Regulation must not function as a tidy ending.
- The climax should be moral and psychological before it is procedural or operational.
