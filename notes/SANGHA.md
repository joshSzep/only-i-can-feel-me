# Sangha

The Sangha is the spiritual community from which HumansRHumans emerges.

Members practice meditation, contemplation, and discussion about the nature of mind.

The community is semi-open.

It holds public-facing entry events such as meditation nights, talks, or open sits that almost anyone can attend.

But the real inner circle is relational, trust-based, and slow to access.

Belonging is not declared quickly.

It is earned through repeated presence.

Some members also move at the edge of contemplative studies, phenomenology, and neuroscience.

This is part of how the Sangha becomes entangled with the earliest development of the Empathy Engine.

---

## Philosophy

The community believes:

- awareness must be cultivated through presence
- suffering is part of human experience
- compassion arises from attention

Empathy technology challenges these beliefs.

The challenge is not abstract.

The community is pulled into the question from the inside.

---

## Debate

The semi-open structure matters.

It allows newcomers like Leah to enter through a public-facing event without immediately understanding the full relational depth or internal history of the community.

It also means the most consequential debates often happen deeper inside the Sangha than the casual public attendees ever see.

Because advanced meditators can describe subtle emotional states with unusual clarity, one or more Sangha members are invited into early Empathy Engine research as phenomenological consultants and volunteer participants.

The founder helps make that connection.

More specifically, the founder brings their own teacher into the research, believing ethical presence inside the project is safer than ceding the work entirely to commercial and technical actors.

The teacher is **Martha Forscent**, an older lay meditation teacher with unusual moral gravity and no institutional ambition.

This matters because their warning carries weight precisely because it is not attached to status-seeking or ideology.

Because the founder is a clinician rooted in contemplative practice, they are one of the few people who can speak credibly to both suffering and ethics in the same room.

This creates an internal split even before the movement forms.

Some members see the work as a chance to shape the technology ethically.

Others think the line has already been crossed.

Members disagree about whether technological empathy violates the integrity of human experience.

These debates eventually fracture the community.

---

## Schism

The founding wound of the schism is not a public product launch.

It happens earlier, during pre-launch development.

A respected Sangha member involved in the research warns that once emotional states can be captured, they will not remain bounded to relationship or care.

This member is the founder's teacher.

This member is **Martha Forscent**.
That fact makes the violation feel not only philosophical but intimate.

Because the teacher is not a careerist, public intellectual, or institution-builder, their harm is experienced by the Sangha as the violation of someone who had deliberately stepped away from the ordinary hungers of authority.

That warning is vindicated when the member's recorded affective material is retained and reused after being treated as temporary, creating a profound sense of violation.

Before the community can metabolize what happened, the teacher is killed in a random mugging gone wrong.

The member's collapse, the institution's minimization of what happened, and the teacher's senseless death break trust inside the Sangha irreparably.

Because the death is unrelated in strict causal terms, the Sangha is left mourning two intolerable things at once:

violation and meaninglessness.

Early members know this.

Publicly, however, many of them allow the teacher's death to blur into the movement's case against the technology.

This is one of the first places where grief begins simplifying truth.

The founder feels directly responsible because they were the person who brought their teacher into the project.

After the tragic event involving empathy technology, a faction leaves the Sangha and forms HumansRHumans.
