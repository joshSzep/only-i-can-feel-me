# Recruit Arc

The recruit is the emotional center of *Only I Can Feel Me*.

The novel is fundamentally the story of their **gradual radicalization and eventual disillusionment**.

The reader should experience the movement through the recruit's perspective.

The recruit must be sympathetic and psychologically believable.

They should not begin as an extremist.

They should begin as someone searching for something meaningful.

Working version of the recruit:

**Leah Serrano**, 31, lives alone in Los Angeles after the collapse of a six-year relationship and works in trust and safety for a major platform, reviewing invasive media and the public degradation of private feeling.

She is carrying unresolved grief after her mother's death and has begun to feel that modern life no longer knows how to hold suffering without turning it into spectacle, product, or policy.

This gives her both the hunger that draws her toward the Sangha and the digital skills that later make her useful to HumansRHumans.

---

# Stage 1 — Disorientation

Leah begins in a period of instability.

The instability is not dramatic in an obvious way.

It is cumulative.

Key factors include:

- loneliness after the end of a long relationship
- unresolved grief after her mother's death
- burnout from reviewing invasive and emotionally corrosive material for work
- growing disgust with a culture that treats interior life as consumable content
- exhaustion, insomnia, and spiritual numbness

She feels disconnected from the world.

She is searching for something grounded and authentic.

---

# Stage 2 — Discovery

Leah encounters the Sangha community.

The community is semi-open.

She can enter through public-facing meditation nights, talks, or open sits.

But the real inner circle is relational and slow to access.

The atmosphere is calm, thoughtful, contemplative.

Members speak about:

- awareness
- suffering
- attention
- compassion
- the nature of mind

For the first time in a long while, Leah feels:

- seen
- grounded
- welcomed

The Sangha becomes a place of refuge.

What especially affects her is that no one demands immediate self-disclosure.

The room respects silence.

The community also does not overclaim her quickly.

She is allowed to remain at the edge for a while.

This makes the Sangha feel less like recruitment than relief.

---

# Stage 3 — Philosophical Awakening

The debates about empathy technology begin.

They are sharpened by the fact that the issue is not remote.

A respected Sangha member has been involved in early Empathy Engine development and brings firsthand moral alarm back into the community.

This person is also the founder's teacher, which gives the debate emotional authority and makes the founder's role morally complicated rather than pure.

They are **Martha Forscent**, an older lay meditation teacher with unusual moral gravity and no institutional ambition.

That lack of ambition matters.

Leah experiences them as one of the few people in her world who does not seem to be converting seriousness into status.

The founder's professional identity as a couples therapist also gives the debate unusual credibility for Leah.

This is not a movement being led by a crank or outsider.

It is being shaped by someone from the world of care.

That professional background also makes the argument for the technology emotionally legible rather than strawman-simple.

If anyone should understand the temptation of a tool that promises to reduce relational misreading, it is a couples therapist.

Leah is also moved by the founder's interpersonal contrast.

In one-on-one settings the founder feels patient, humane, and non-coercive.

In group settings they become unexpectedly authoritative.

This makes belonging feel both emotionally safe and morally serious.

Members discuss questions like:

- should machines mediate human emotional experience?
- is empathy something that should be technologically transmitted?
- does this violate the boundary of consciousness?

Leah becomes fascinated by these discussions.

They resonate with what her life already feels like:

that human feeling is increasingly being extracted, circulated, managed, and consumed without dignity.

The HumansRHumans philosophy feels intellectually serious and morally important.

---

# Stage 4 — The Fracture

The tragic event involving empathy technology occurs.

Specifically, a Sangha member who helped with early phenomenology and calibration work discovers that their recorded emotional material has been retained and reused after being treated as temporary.

The founder is the one who brought this teacher into the work.

The member had warned that once feelings could be captured, they would no longer remain sovereign.

Now the warning has become fact.

The teacher dies not long afterward.

The death is a random mugging gone wrong.

It is unrelated to the technology in direct causal terms, which makes the resulting grief harder, not easier, to metabolize.

This event emotionally shocks the community.

What follows is not only grief but betrayal.

The institution treats the event as a technical or consent problem.

The Sangha experiences it as desecration.

The teacher's death becomes the movement's deepest wound.

The fact that the death is senseless and not cleanly attributable to the technology creates room for grief, guilt, and myth to merge.

Early members know the death was unrelated street violence.

Publicly, however, they allow it to blur into the movement's indictment of the technology.

This gives Leah an early experience of something dangerous:

a cause that feels morally right beginning to accept a cleaner story than truth permits.

Some members believe the technology must now be actively resisted.

Leah sympathizes with this position.

HumansRHumans begins to form.

She joins.

---

# Stage 5 — Belonging

The movement provides the recruit with something powerful:

purpose.

Leah feels part of something meaningful.

She begins participating in:

- meetings
- moderation of online spaces
- filtering newcomers
- deciding who seems sincere, reckless, unserious, or dangerous
- shaping access to deeper channels
- messaging and online communication

Because of her work background, she quickly becomes useful in the movement's digital life.

She helps moderate channels, shape language, and filter out people who seem unserious or reckless.

Very quickly, she becomes part of a quiet gatekeeping function.

She is not just joining the movement.

She is helping decide who gets to belong inside it.

Her identity becomes increasingly tied to the movement.

---

# Stage 6 — Escalation

As the movement grows online, new factions emerge.

The first serious distortion comes from a sabotage faction that argues empathy technology is an existential threat.

From that premise, they argue that damage is justified.

Some members advocate increasingly extreme tactics.

At first Leah rationalizes these developments.

Because she helped shape access to the movement's inner digital life, she feels implicated in who got in and what tone those spaces developed.

The sabotage faction does not initially present itself as bloodthirsty.

It presents itself as morally adult.

It claims to be the only faction willing to accept the true scale of the threat.

Its first serious action is intimidation and targeted harassment of researchers or clinicians associated with empathy technology.

Because this is framed as pressure rather than outright violence, it is easier for Leah and others to rationalize.

Leah is implicated directly but not cleanly.

She moderates the channels where the campaign is coordinated and morally cleaned up.

She removes some of the most explicit excesses, which allows her to feel responsible.

But she does not shut the campaign down.

In practice, that means she helps create the conditions under which it continues.

She believes:

the cause is too important to fail.

She begins accepting compromises she once would have rejected.

---

# Stage 7 — Doubt

Leah begins noticing:

- ideological simplification
- growing hostility
- disregard for nuance
- rhetoric replacing philosophy

She begins questioning whether the movement still resembles its original ideals.

But leaving would mean losing:

- community
- purpose
- identity

It would also mean admitting that the first place that made her feel less spiritually alone has become part of the problem.

So she stays.

---

# Stage 8 — Confrontation

Leah eventually speaks with the founder.

The founder expresses concern about the direction the movement has taken.

By this point Leah also understands that the founder's authority is inseparable from unresolved guilt toward the teacher they helped expose.

She realizes the founder no longer fully controls the movement.

This conversation plants the seed of clarity.

But it is not yet final.

At this stage Leah can still tell herself that moral authority might recover practical force.

---

# Stage 9 — The Political Victory

Empathy technology becomes regulated.

The movement's original goal has effectively been achieved.

Leah expects celebration or dissolution.

Instead the movement intensifies.

Some factions now argue the technology must be eliminated entirely.

The founder attempts to narrow, pause, or redirect the movement.

Leah sees that these efforts no longer matter in practice.

People still quote the founder.

They no longer obey them.

What makes this irreversible is not only disobedience.

Leah watches members plan a new campaign of threats framed as warnings even after regulation has already achieved the movement's original political aim.

The target is **Kate Minform**, an essayist who publicly defends regulated empathy technology.

Leah knows Kate only at a distance through her essays and public appearances, which makes the campaign feel at once abstracted and unmistakably real.

The novel stays on Leah's side of that distance rather than showing Kate directly receiving or processing the harassment.

Part of what makes Kate intolerable to the movement is her calm moral tone in support of the technology, which feels to them more dangerous than open triumphalism.

The form this first takes on the page is revealing: threats framed as warnings, which let participants describe coercion as conscience.

The language itself is intimate moral accusation, written as if Kate has betrayed her own soul rather than simply argued for the wrong public settlement.

What makes the moment worse is that the first version Leah sees does not come from an anonymous channel participant.

It comes from **Second Bell**, a known splinter leader, which gives the rhetoric sanction and makes clear how far leadership has drifted.

Part of what gives the rhetoric its force is Second Bell's austere presence in the channels.

They make escalation feel disciplined rather than impulsive.

The rhetoric often arrives in numbered doctrinal posts, which makes each escalation read like the next necessary inference rather than a fresh decision.

At the sentence level, the posts feel coldly logical, which makes Leah's disgust slower and more dangerous.

This leader is not one of Leah's old anchors in the movement.

They are a later figure she mainly knows through the movement's channels, which underscores how much authority now flows through mediated presence rather than earned intimacy.

They invoke the founder's name, language, and moral authority while doing the opposite of what the founder is explicitly asking for.

Their doctrine is simple and corrosive:

the founder cared about interior sovereignty, and true sovereignty allows no compromise.

---

# Stage 10 — Realization

Leah finally understands.

The movement is no longer fighting for its original goal.

It is sustaining itself.

The ideology has become self-perpetuating.

Her irreversible clarity comes when she sees that the founder's authority has become largely ceremonial.

The person whose moral seriousness once held the movement together can no longer stop what the movement does in their name.

The movement does not need to reject the founder openly.

It has become more dangerous than that.

It can preserve the founder as a symbol while discarding the founder as a restraint.

She recognizes the tragic truth:

The movement meant to defend humanity has lost the humanity it was meant to protect.

---

# Final Emotional State

Leah does not end as a triumphant hero.

She ends with clarity.

She understands:

- why the movement began
- why people joined
- why it changed
- why the founder can no longer retrieve it
- why it will likely continue

Her final realization is both sobering and deeply human.
