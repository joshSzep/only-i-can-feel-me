# Only I Can Feel Me

![Only I Can Feel Me Cover](cover.png)

**Only I Can Feel Me** is a philosophical science-fiction novel by **Joshua Szepietowski** set in the same universe as *What It Feels Like to Be You*.

Where the first novel explores the promise and danger of **empathy technology**, this companion story explores the **human resistance to it**.

It follows the rise of a decentralized underground movement known as **HumansRHumans** — a group that believes the boundary between human consciousness and machines must never be crossed.

This book examines how a movement can grow from compassion, become radicalized through fear and tragedy, and eventually evolve into something its founders never intended.

## Premise

When technology emerges that allows one person to experience the emotional state of another, humanity stands on the edge of a profound transformation.

Some see it as the greatest tool for compassion ever created.

Others see it as the ultimate violation of human interiority.

Out of a meditation community shaken by tragedy, a movement forms:

**HumansRHumans**

Their belief is simple.

> Only humans should experience human consciousness.  
> Machines must never cross that boundary.

What begins as a philosophical resistance grows into a decentralized underground movement spreading across the internet.

Cells form. Ideology spreads. Interpretations diverge.

And as the world moves toward regulating empathy technology, the movement begins to evolve beyond its original purpose.

---

## Themes

This novel explores questions such as:

- What does it mean to **experience another person's inner life**?
- Is empathy something that can be **technologically mediated**?
- Where does the **boundary of the self** truly lie?
- How do movements change once they **escape their founders**?
- Can noble intentions survive the logic of **"ends justify the means"**?

---

## Relationship to the First Book

The two novels form a philosophical pair.

| Book | Focus |
|-----|------|
| **What It Feels Like to Be You** | The creation and promise of empathy technology |
| **Only I Can Feel Me** | The resistance to crossing the human–machine boundary |

One explores **shared experience**.

The other explores the **defense of interiority**.
