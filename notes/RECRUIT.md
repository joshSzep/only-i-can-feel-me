# The Recruit

The recruit is the central point-of-view character.

Working version:

**Leah Serrano**, 31.

She lives alone in Los Angeles after the end of a six-year relationship and works in trust and safety for a major platform, spending her days reviewing invasive media, private suffering turned into content, and the public fallout surrounding empathy technology.

She is not a natural activist.

She is a tired, serious person whose life has become emotionally procedural.

## Temperament

Leah is:

- observant
- restrained
- conflict-avoidant
- quietly compassionate
- skeptical of easy belonging
- more comfortable listening than declaring
- ashamed of how badly she wants a place to belong

## Wounds

Leah is carrying several forms of disorientation:

- her mother died after a long illness that made care feel administrative and depersonalized
- she never fully metabolized that grief
- her long relationship collapsed partly because she became emotionally unavailable inside it
- her work has left her feeling that modern life turns even private suffering into traffic, policy, and spectacle

She encounters HumansRHumans during a period of personal vulnerability.

The movement speaks to genuine needs in her before it becomes ideology.

Key motivations:

- loneliness
- grief
- disillusionment
- moral confusion
- search for belonging

She does not enter the Sangha because she already has a political program.

She enters because her life feels spiritually thinned out.

## Why the Sangha Feels Like Relief

The Sangha offers Leah things she no longer trusts the wider world to provide:

- silence that does not feel empty
- attention that does not feel extractive
- ritual without branding
- language for suffering that is neither clinical nor performative
- a community that respects distance rather than trying to collapse it

The movement initially feels like refuge.

It provides:

- community
- clarity
- purpose
- identity

This matters.

HumansRHumans will eventually radicalize her partly because it first gives her a believable experience of being held.

## Why She Becomes Useful

Leah's digital fluency and emotional restraint make her useful early in the movement.

Her core role becomes moderating HumansRHumans' online spaces and filtering members.

She can:

- moderate discussions
- screen newcomers
- distinguish serious members from thrill-seekers, opportunists, and unstable actors
- enforce tone and ideological boundaries in digital spaces
- help decide who gets access to deeper channels and real-world trust
- organize communications around those spaces
- draft careful language when public messaging is needed

This usefulness deepens her identification with the movement.

It also makes her one of the people who quietly shapes what kind of movement HumansRHumans becomes.

Her first serious moral compromise emerges here.

When the sabotage current begins coordinating harassment and intimidation against researchers or clinicians, Leah is one of the people moderating the channels where it is planned, softened, and rationalized.

She does not fully stop it.

Instead, she tells herself she is containing worse behavior while allowing the campaign to continue.

---

## Radicalization Arc

1. curiosity
2. participation
3. loyalty
4. ideological commitment
5. moral compromise
6. doubt
7. realization

The recruit’s final realization is that the movement has become detached from its original purpose.
